Alleviating Eco-Anxiety in Youth

A review of the literature on eco-anxiety culminating with a speculative, community-led, nature-based, multi-sensory intervention.

Eco-anxiety is a largely comtemporary phenomenon, defined by psycological distress as a result of the ongoing crises and uncertainty of human-cause climate change. For this project, I conducted an extensive literature review on the topic, including mentions of eco-anxiety in popular media. From there, I explored numerous potential interventions before settling on the development a workshop concept. In this workshop, adults and youth together spend time in nature, using found objects such as twigs, leaves, flowers, stones etc, to create a visual representation the sounds they hear around them. In the forest in particular, the unique sonic signature of collective bird song in any one location may be used to ground oneself in the fleeting, yet inherently beautiful and grounding experience of place among non-human species.

The practice of paying attention in this circumstance, using wild objects to generate a physical representation of this space in time, enables a deep connection to nature while appreciating the beauty in the ephemeral. In theory, taking solace in this moment through such a practice, can reduce eco-anxiety, similar to traditional methods of mental health treatment where reducing rumination on the past or future can promote psycological wellness.
